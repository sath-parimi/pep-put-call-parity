Empirical Analysis of Put-Call Parity on PepsiCo Options

Goal: Demonstrate how put-call parity links theoretical Black-Scholes prices to market option quotes using PepsiCo (PEP) as the underlying stock.

Approach

  1. Stock context: Reviewed Pepsi's price history and log returns as of September 11, 2025.
  2. Theoretical parity: Verified that $C - P = S e^{-qT} - K e^{-rT}$
    holds under the Black-Scholes model with dividends across a range of strikes.
  3. Market parity: Pulled the option chain expiring September 12, 2025, computed $C-P$ from mid quotes, and compared observed deviations against a bid-ask tolerance band.

Key choices

  • This notebook was run after market hours on September 11, 2025, which automatically synced ASOF to that date and selected the nearest expiry (September 12, 2025). Running after hours pulls end-of-day prices and option quotes, which avoids intraday noise and ensures reproducibility of figures.
  • Applied continuous compounding rates $(r, q)$, with dividend yield $q$ on the stock leg.
  • End-of-day option chain data showed consistent pricing, with no major issues from illiquidity or missing quotes.

#Remove time zone for safe datetime comparisons
def _drop_tz(df: pd.DataFrame) -> pd.DataFrame:
    if not isinstance(df.index, pd.DatetimeIndex):
        return df
    try:
        df = df.copy()
        df.index = df.index.tz_localize(None)   #works if tz-aware
    except TypeError:
        #already tz-naive; nothing to do
        pass
    return df

#Retrieve Pepsi's spot price (S0) as of the pinned ASOF date.
#If the date is not a trading day, the function falls back to the most recent close.
def get_spot_asof(ticker: str, asof_str: str) -> float:
    asof = datetime.fromisoformat(asof_str)
    start = (asof - timedelta(days=7)).strftime("%Y-%m-%d")
    end   = (asof + timedelta(days=1)).strftime("%Y-%m-%d")
    hist = yf.Ticker(ticker).history(start=start, end=end)
    hist = _drop_tz(hist)
    cutoff = pd.to_datetime(asof_str)          #tz-naive cutoff
    hist = hist[hist.index <= cutoff]
    if hist.empty:
        raise ValueError("No close found on/before ASOF; adjust ASOF/window.")
    return float(hist["Close"].iloc[-1])

Part 2: Theoretical Put-Call Parity (Black-Scholes with dividends)

Motivation: Before testing against market data, it is important to confirm that put-call parity holds in theory. This ensures our implementation of Black-Scholes pricing with dividends is consistent and that any later deviations reflect market conditions, not coding errors. We will verify that the parity relation holds to within numerical precision, which in this case is on the order of $10^{-14}$.

Method: Calculated the year fraction between September 11, 2025 (ASOF date) and September 12, 2025 (expiry). Using Pepsi's spot price and fixed values for the risk-free rate and dividend yield, priced calls and puts across a range of strikes with Black-Scholes. Then compared the left-hand ($C-P$) and right-hand ($S e^{-qT} - K e^{-rT}$) sides of the parity relation across strikes.

#Part 2: Theoretical PutCall Parity (BlackScholes with dividends)

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from datetime import datetime

#Year fraction (ACT/365) between ASOF and EXPIRY for pricing horizon T
def yearfrac(asof_str: str, expiry_str: str) -> float:
    asof = datetime.fromisoformat(asof_str)
    exp  = datetime.fromisoformat(expiry_str)
    return (exp - asof).days / 365.0

T = yearfrac(ASOF, EXPIRY)

#BlackScholes European call/put with continuous dividend yield q
def bs_call_put(S: float, K: float, r: float, q: float, T: float, sigma: float):
    """
    Returns (C, P) under BlackScholes with continuous dividend yield q.
    Note: Volatility affects C and P but not whether the parity identity holds.
    """
    if T <= 0 or sigma <= 0 or S <= 0 or K <= 0:
        return (np.nan, np.nan)
    d1 = (np.log(S / K) + (r - q + 0.5 * sigma**2) * T) / (sigma * np.sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sigma * np.sqrt(T)
    C  = S * np.exp(-q * T) * norm.cdf(d1) - K * np.exp(-r * T) * norm.cdf(d2)
    P  = K * np.exp(-r * T) * norm.cdf(-d2) - S * np.exp(-q * T) * norm.cdf(-d1)
    return (C, P)

#Build a strike grid around S0 and evaluate parity components under BS
SIGMA_THEORY = 0.25  #placeholder volatility for pricing visualization
K_grid = np.linspace(0.7 * S0, 1.3 * S0, 25)

rows = []
for K in K_grid:
    C, P = bs_call_put(S0, K, r, q, T, SIGMA_THEORY)
    lhs = C - P
    rhs = S0 * np.exp(-q * T) - K * np.exp(-r * T)
    rows.append({
        "strike": K,
        "C": C, "P": P,
        "lhs_C_minus_P": lhs,
        "rhs_Se_qT_minus_Ke_rT": rhs,
        "diff": lhs - rhs
    })

df_theory = pd.DataFrame(rows).sort_values("strike")
display(df_theory.head(8))

#Plot parity difference vs strike.
#This should be ~0 up to numerical precision.
fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.axhline(0.0, linestyle="--", linewidth=1)
ax.plot(df_theory["strike"], df_theory["diff"])
ax.set_title(f"Figure 3: Theoretical Parity Difference vs Strike\n({TICKER}, expiry {EXPIRY})")
ax.set_xlabel("Strike")
ax.set_ylabel(r"$C - P - (S e^{-qT} - K e^{-rT})$")
plt.show()

Part 3: Market Put-Call Parity

Motivation: After confirming parity in theory, the next step is to see how well it lines up with actual quotes. Any differences mainly reflect trading frictions like bid-ask, stale prints, or illiquidity.

Method: Pulled the live option chain for PEP from September 11, 2025 with expiration on September 12, 2025. Computed mid prices for calls and puts at each strike ($C_{mid} - P_{mid}$) and compared it to the theoretical right side ($S e^{-qT} - K e^{-rT}$). The orange dotted line represents the upper tolerance (+ band) and the green dotted line the lower tolerance (- band), which are the sum of call and put half-spreads.

#Part 3: Market Put-Call Parity

T = yearfrac(ASOF, EXPIRY)
tk = yf.Ticker(TICKER)

#Pull option chain (calls & puts) for the chosen live expiry
chain = tk.option_chain(EXPIRY)
calls_raw = chain.calls.copy()
puts_raw  = chain.puts.copy()

#Raw mids
calls_raw["C_mid"] = (calls_raw["bid"] + calls_raw["ask"]) / 2.0
puts_raw["P_mid"]  = (puts_raw["bid"]  + puts_raw["ask"])  / 2.0

#Half-spreads for a visual tolerance band
calls_raw["C_halfspread"] = (calls_raw["ask"] - calls_raw["bid"]) / 2.0
puts_raw["P_halfspread"]  = (puts_raw["ask"]  - puts_raw["bid"])  / 2.0

#Merge by strike
m = pd.merge(
    calls_raw[["strike", "C_mid", "C_halfspread"]],
    puts_raw[["strike", "P_mid", "P_halfspread"]],
    on="strike", how="inner"
).sort_values("strike")

#Parity components
m["lhs_mid"] = m["C_mid"] - m["P_mid"]
m["rhs_theory"] = S0 * np.exp(-q * T) - m["strike"] * np.exp(-r * T)
m["diff"] = m["lhs_mid"] - m["rhs_theory"]
m["band"] = (m["C_halfspread"] + m["P_halfspread"]).astype(float)

Conclusion

Project Findings: This analysis verified put-call parity on PepsiCo options from September 11-12, 2025.

  • In Part 1, we reviewed Pepsi's price history and daily log returns, establishing context for option valuation.
  • In Part 2, we found that theoretical parity holds to numerical precision, confirming that the Black-Scholes model with dividends enforced the relationship exactly.
  • In Part 3, we compared theoretical put-call parity to live market quotes. The observed differences fluctuated around zero but remained within the tolerance band implied by bid-ask spreads.

Interpretation: Since the blue line (market diff) stayed between the orange and green dotted bands, the market is consistent with put-call parity once transaction costs are considered. If the blue line had fallen outside these bands, that would signal a potential arbitrage opportunity to construct a risk-free profit by trading stock, calls, and puts. In practice, however, such violations are rare and fleeting. They are usually eliminated quickly by market makers or high-frequency traders, or absorbed into wider spreads that reflect trading frictions.

Takeaway: Pepsi's option market is consistent with put-call parity. What look like "violations" are better understood as the normal effects of transaction costs, liquidity, and spreads rather than exploitable arbitrage.
